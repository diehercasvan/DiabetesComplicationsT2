2. Epidemiology

Data from IDF Atlas indicates that 415 million adults have diabetes in 2015. By 2040 this will rise to 642 million. The North America and Caribbean Region has the highest prevalence of diabetes compared to the other IDF regions with 12.9% of the adult population affected and the highest prevalence of impaired glucose tolerance (IGT). One adult in eight in the North America and Caribbean Region has diabetes. A further 51.8 million people, or 15.0% of adults aged 20-79 in the North America and Caribbean Region have impaired glucose tolerance, putting them at high risk of developing type 2 diabetes. By 2040 it is estimated that 60.5 million adults will be living with diabetes and a further 67.4 million will have impaired glucose tolerance.3

The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that in 2004, 3,4 million people died as a consequence of diabetes.5 Eighty percent of deaths occur in low and middle-income countries.5 Around 50% of deaths occur in patients under age 70 and 55% are women.5

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is the third leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide. In developing countries, DR is the leading cause of irreversible blindness in working-age people (age 16 to 64).4

DR is responsible for 5% of 37 million of blindness cases globally.4

Between 20% and 30% of diabetic patients will have some degree of DR at some point in their lives.4

Five percent of people with diabetes will have vision-threatening DR (proliferative DR or diabetic macular edema).4

Between 3% and 10% of diabetic patients will have diabetic macular edema with visual impairment.4 The 0,5% of diabetics will require vitrectomy surgery for the treatment of DR.4
