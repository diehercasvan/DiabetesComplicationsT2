7. Diagnosis

All diabetic patients should be included into a screening program for DR.4

Detection methods for DR (Figure 5).

  • Fundus by non-ophthalmologist: it is done without pupillary dilation and using a direct ophthalmoscope; It is a test of low sensitivity and specificity.4
  • Fundus performed by the ophthalmologist: it is the examination of choice, and is performed with slit lamp with magnifier and dilated pupil. It has higher sensitivity and specificity.4
  • Fundus with cameras: a cost-effective examination with sensitivity and specificity similar to those of the fundus performed by the ophthalmologist.4

The ophthalmic examination includes: best corrected visual acuity, assessment of intraocular pressure, biomicroscopy, gonioscopy (when indicated), and fundus under mydriasis (examination of the vitreous, posterior pole and peripheral retina).10

Other diagnostic methods include:

  • Fluorescein angiography: it is not a routine test, and is indicated to support the modification of laser treatment or when there is an uncertain diagnosis.10 It identifies leakage sites, and is useful in cases of macula with no perfusion and to evaluate signs of macular ischemia.10
  • Ultrasound: is useful for diabetics with opaque ocular media which impedes the evaluation using the ophthalmoscope.7,10
  • Optical coherence tomography: useful in patients with diabetic macular edema, in monitoring and to identify points of vitreomacular traction; in patients who do not respond to laser therapy or in patients in whom vitrectomy with hyaloid removal is under consideration.7,10
