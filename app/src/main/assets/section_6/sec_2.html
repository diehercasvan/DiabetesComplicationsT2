3. Pathophysiology

he duration and magnitude of hyperglycemia are directly related to microangiopathy development.1 Endothelial cells are especially affected by this condition.1 Massive glucose entry into endothelial cell leads to increased permeability, release of growth factors, and retinal capillary vasoconstriction.1

Mechanisms involved in cell damage

  • Increased polyol pathway flux: aldose reductase normally has the function of reducing toxic aldehydes to inactive alcohols.6 When the glucose concentration in the cell becomes too high, this enzyme reduces that glucose to sorbitol, which is later oxidized to fructose.6 This process consumes the cofactor NADPH, which is a critical antioxidant with regenerative functions. NADPH reduction increases susceptibility to intracellular oxidative stress.1,6
  • Intracellular production of advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) precursors: non-enzymatic glycation of protein amino groups (-NH2), which depends on blood glucose levels and leads to formation of Schiff bases and Amadori products, disrupts the normal functioning of glycated proteins.1 AGE precursors can diffuse into the cell, altering gene transcription. These AGE precursors can also diffuse out of the cell and modify extracellular matrix molecules, which causes cellular dysfunction. AGE precursors modify circulating proteins in the blood (such as albumin), increasing the production of cytokines and growth factors, which cause microvascular pathology.6
  • Protein kinase-C isoforms activation: a significant increase in protein kinase-C (PKC) levels has been observed in retinal endothelial cell cultures.1 As a consequence of hyperglycemia, the levels of intracellular diacylglycerol increase, which leads to activation of PKC isoforms alpha, beta and epsilon.1 The abnormal activation of PKC affects endothelial nitric oxide synthase, and increases the synthesis of endothelin-1 (ET-1), vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β1), plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1), nuclear factor Kβ (NF-Kβ) and NADPH oxidase.1,6 The final consequences of these processes are: blood flow abnormalities, angiogenesis and increased vascular permeability, capillary occlusion, and increased expression of proinflammatory genes.6
  • Increased flux of hexosamines: some of fructose 6-phosphate, derived from glycolysis inside the endothelial cell, is destined to production of uridine diphosphate N-acetyl glucosamine, which in turn is an important source of growth factors and plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1).1
    Intracellular hyperglycemia increases the mitochondrial production of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which in turn increases the activity of poly ADP ribose polymerase (PARP). The action of glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) decreases and, finally, aforementioned metabolic pathways are activated, leading to microvascular damage.6
