2. Epidemiology

Data from IDF Atlas indicates that 415 million adults have diabetes in 2015. By 2040 this will rise to 642 million. The North America and Caribbean Region has the highest prevalence of diabetes compared to the other IDF regions with 12.9% of the adult population affected and the highest prevalence of impaired glucose tolerance (IGT).3

Diabetic foot is considered a public health concern due to its high prevalence and its high economic and social burden.2

About 20% of patients with diabetes will develop a foot ulcer at some point in their lives.2

Diabetes is the cause of 50% to 90% of non-traumatic lower limb amputations.2

In patients with diabetes hospitalized for foot ulcers, follow-up has shown that:

  • During the year following the first ulcer, 34% have a new ulcer and 3% have an amputation with mortality rates between 13% and 40%.2
  • After 3 years, 61% have new ulcers; 10% have amputations and mortality is between 35% and 65%.2
  • After 5 years, 70% have new ulcers; 12% have amputations and mortality is between 39% and 80%.2

Currently, studies in the Latin American population about diabetic foot prevalence, incidence, disability, lost work days, and hospitalization costs are scarce and inconclusive.4
