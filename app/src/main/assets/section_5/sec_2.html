3. Pathophysiology

Diabetic foot is a neuropathy-based disorder induced by sustained hyperglycemia that, with or without concomitant ischemia and after a traumatic trigger, eventually generates a foot injury or ulcer.5

The three cornerstones in the pathophysiology of diabetic foot are:

  • Neuropathy5
  • Ischemia5
  • Infection5

a. Diabetic neuropathy
Diabetic neuropathy can be present in up to 50% of diabetics over 60 years.4

Distal symmetric polyneuropathy (DSP) is the most common form of diabetic neuropathy.6 DSP involves motor and sensory fibers, thereby altering sensory, motor and autonomic functions.6

The main clinical manifestation of DSP is sensory loss.7

Sensory neuropathy is established as the cause of injuries through external trauma (burn produced by a hot-water bottle, wound when walking barefoot, inappropriate footwear that exerts pressure, etc.) that is not perceived by the patient due to alterations of the protective sensation.4

Motor neuropathy is responsible for internal trauma, which encompasses the acquired deformities of the feet and alterations in plantar pressure (plantar overpressure, claw toes or hammertoes, hallux, plantar hyperkeratosis, etc.).4

Autonomic neuropathy facilitates fissure formation and entry of germs that may lead to infection.5

b. Ischemia

Microcirculation
The role of microcirculation in ulcer genesis and wound healing in diabetic patients is controversial.8

Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) Peripheral arterial disease (secondary to atherosclerosis) is a common condition in patients with diabetes.9
The annual incidence of PAD for patients with type 1 diabetes is 5,5/1000 patients, and 13,6/1000 for patients with type 2 diabetes.9
Atherosclerosis in diabetic patients tends to occur at younger ages. It is usually more widespread and affect distal vessels.9

In diabetic patients, the risk of ulceration and amputation increases in the presence of peripheral arterial disease.9 Patients with diabetes have 2 to 4 times higher rates of peripheral arterial disease.10 In diabetic population, infrapopliteal arterial occlusive disease is more common.10

c. Infection
In diabetic patients, inflammatory response disturbances have been described, especially in:

  • Diapedesis mechanisms5
  • Leukocyte adhesion5
  • Chemotaxis5
  • Destructive capacity of polymorphonuclear cells5

The three phases of the inflammatory response (increased blood supply, hyperproteic exudate accumulation and collagen formation) may be altered in the diabetic patient.5

The decrease in blood supply, which causes hypoxia, and alterations in the vascular response promote the growth of anaerobic bacteria and modify the local self-regulatory mechanisms.5
