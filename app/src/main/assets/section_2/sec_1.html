2.DIABETES MELLITUS AND CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE

In order to establish an association between diabetes mellitus (DM) and cardiovascular disease (CVD), we must remember certain findings demonstrated by several clinical trials:

  • The leading cause of death in diabetic patients is cardiovascular disease (coronary heart disease, stroke and peripheral vascular disease).7
  • Eighty percent of deaths among diabetic population are attributable to macrovascular complications of the disease.7
  • The incidence and prevalence of coronary heart disease among patients with diabetes (type 1 and type 2) are increasing; this is explained by a greater burden of cardiovascular risk factors in this population (essential hypertension, dyslipidemia and obesity).7
  • Patients with DM have a risk of coronary heart disease 2 to 4 times higher than nondiabetic patients.7
  • In women with diabetes mellitus, the risk of CVD increases 3 to 5 times. In men, this risk increases 2 to 3 times.8
  • The risk of acute myocardial infarction in diabetic patients is 2 to 4 times higher than in patients without DM.7
  • The risk of heart failure is between 2 and 8 times higher in diabetic patients.7
  • DM patients with no prior history of ischemic heart disease have the same risk of cardiac events than non-diabetic patients with previous history of MI (this concept leads to consider diabetes as a coronary equivalent).7,8
  • In patients with acute coronary syndrome, diabetes is found in up to 45% of them; In some studies, 25% had undiagnosed DM and 40% had alterations in fasting plasma glucose.8
  • The course of acute coronary events is worse in patients with DM; an increase between 25% and 100% in MI inpatients mortality has been shown.7
  • The long-term mortality in patients with DM after a MI is increased 2 to 3 times.7
  • The risk of sudden cardiac death is higher in people with diabetes, being higher in women.7
  • In patients with DM of several years of evolution, silent ischemia and myocardial infarction without chest pain are more common.7
  • There is a direct and continuous relationship between hyperglycemia and CVD. The higher the levels of glycosylated hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c), the higher the risk for CVD.8
  • Postprandial glucose levels are associated with CVD risk, even in patients with normal fasting glucose.8
