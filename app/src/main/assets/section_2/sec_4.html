5. Symptoms

Patients with angina may report:

  • Pain: Typical pain begins gradually, reaching a peak within first 5 to 20 minutes, and is relieved with rest or medication.14 Pain makes the patient stops for resting.14 Pain lasting more than 20 minutes requires considering an acute coronary syndrome.14 The pain is retrosternal; the patient indicates pain location through open and clenched hand placed on the chest (Levine sign).14 The pain may radiate to the upper left limb, neck or jaw; sometimes epigastric pain occurs.14
  • Other symptoms: sweating, paleness, nausea and dyspnea (with exercise or at rest).14 Some older patients may report fatigue, belching or fainting.14
  • The pain may be triggered by physical exertion, stress, exposure to cold, exposure to smoke, hearty meal or isometric exercise.14

Twenty percent of patients with chest pain treated in emergency have unstable angina andmyocardial infarction without ST elevation, and 5% have ST elevation MI.15

Patients with unstable angina have a similar pain, but three patterns are distinguished:

  • Angina at rest: episodes of angina lasting more than 20 minutes, in the absence of physical activity.15
  • New onset angina: occurs with little physical effort, limiting daily activity.15
  • Incremental Angina: pattern changes; Frequency, intensity and duration of anginal episodes increase gradually.15

In populations with atypical forms of presentation of angina, including diabetic patients, manifestations may include dyspnea, epigastric pain, fatigue and pain in shoulders and neck.15

Patients with acute myocardial infarction clinically may have (Figure 7):

  • Pain: visceral type (oppressive, crushing, compressive).16
  • Prolonged (> 20 minutes and usually several hours).16
  • No improvement with nitroglycerin.16
  • Usually severe.16
  • Located in the interscapular, retrosternal, precordial region, or anterior chest, or epigastrium.16
  • Radiates to the ulnar area of one or both upper limbs, shoulders, neck or lower jaw.16
  • Associated symptoms: nausea or vomiting, diarrhea or rectal urgency, left heart failure symptoms, weakness, dizziness, palpitations, cold sweats, feeling of distress or death.16

Up to 20% of infarctions occur in silent or atypical form, especially in diabetic patients.16
