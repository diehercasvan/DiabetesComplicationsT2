1. EPIDEMIOLOGY

According to the Pan American Health Organization, in 2013, the main causes of mortality in the Americas were: ischemic heart disease (13,67%), stroke (7,03%), and diabetes mellitus (4,25%).1

In Mexico, according to The National Institute of Statistics and Geography (INEGI), in 2013, the first three causes of overall mortality were: ischemic heart disease, diabetes mellitus and malignancies (Figure 2).2

Data from IDF Atlas shows that 415 million adults have diabetes in 2015. By 2040 this will rise to 642 million. The North America and Caribbean Region has the highest prevalence of diabetes compared to the other IDF regions with 12.9% of the adult population affected and the highest prevalence of impaired glucose tolerance (IGT). One adult in eight in the North America and Caribbean Region has diabetes. A further 51.8 million people, or 15.0% of adults aged 20-79 in the North America and Caribbean Region have impaired glucose tolerance, putting them at high risk of developing type 2 diabetes. By 2040 it is estimated that 60.5 million adults will be living with diabetes and a further 67.4 million will have impaired glucose tolerance.3

The INTERHEART study (Latin America) shows that six specific cardiovascular risk factors are associated with a high risk for acute myocardial infarction (MI): hypertension, persistent stress, diabetes mellitus, smoking, increased waist/hip ratio, and ApoB/ApoA1 ratio.5

In Mexico, between 1993 (ENEC) and 2006 (ENSANUT), a growing trend in the prevalence of cardiovascular risk factors was shown:6

  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus:6,7% → 14,4%
  • Metabolic syndrome:26,6% → 36,8%
  • Hypertension:23,8% → 30,7%
  • Hypercholesterolemia:27% → 43,6%
  • High LDL Cholesterol:31,6% → 46%
  • Low HDL Cholesterol:60,5% →63%

This behavior predicts a short-term increase in morbidity and mortality associated with diabetes mellitus and cardiovascular risk factors.6
