5. Symptoms of painful diabetic neuropathy

Symptoms are usually bilateral, in hands and feet; Unilateral cases eventually progress to become bilateral.1

From a distal localization, painful diabetic neuropathy (PDN) may also involve legs and knees. Typical forms of sensory disturbances in a stocking-glove distribution are not uncommon.1

The main symptom in PDN is pain.1 Sometimes pain is worse at night.1 It can be described as burning, shooting, stabbing, deep, and exacerbated at rest; Less often, it is diffuse and accompanied by paresthesia and cramps.1

Up to 50% of patients with diabetic peripheral polyneuropathy complain of pain, and between 10% and 20% have severe symptoms that require treatment.15

Over time, Achilles reflex, proprioception and vibratory sensation may be affected.1
