3. EPIDEMIOLOGY OF DIABETES AND STROKE IN DIABETIC PATIENTS

According to the International Diabetes Federation (IDF), in 2013, 8.3% of adults worldwide, about 382 million people, had diabetes. Without appropriate interventions, this number will be increased to over 592 million people in 2035, which represents an increase of 55%.7 It is estimated that every 10 minutes, about 3 new cases of diabetes are diagnosed.7

The National Health and Nutrition Survey 2006 (known in Spanish as ENSANUT) estimated that the overall prevalence of diabetes in Mexico was 14% (7,34% of patients had previously diagnosed diabetes; and 7,07% were diagnosed during the survey). According to that data, in 2006 in Mexico, there were 7,3 million people with diabetes.8 Of patients with previously diagnosed disease, 56,2% had a very poor control of their disease; 38.4%, poor control; and only 5.3% had good control of diabetes.8

In Mexico, between 1993 (ENEC) and 2006 (ENSANUT), a growing trend in the prevalence of cardiovascular risk factors was shown:9

  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus:6.7% → 14.4%
  • Metabolic syndrome:26.6% → 36.8%
  • Hypertension:23.8% → 30.7%
  • Hypercholesterolemia: 27% → 43.6%
  • High LDL Cholesterol:31.6% → 46%
  • Low HDL Cholesterol:60.5% → 63%

According to the Pan American Health Organization, in 2013, stroke (7,03%) was the second cause of mortality in the Americas (Figure 2).10

In Mexico, according to The National Institute of Statistics and Geography (INEGI), in 2013, the first three causes of overall mortality were: ischemic heart disease, diabetes mellitus and malignancies (Figure 3).11

Stroke and diabetes
Cardiovascular diseases (stroke, coronary artery disease and peripheral vascular disease) are the leading causes of mortality in patients with diabetes mellitus (DM).12
Eighty percent of deaths among diabetic patients are due to macrovascular manifestations of the disease.12
Risk of stroke of thromboembolic origin is increased in patients with DM, even after adjusting by other associated risk factors.12
Risk of hemorrhagic stroke does not seem to be increased in patients with DM.
Mortality in patients with DM is higher after a cerebral infarction.12
In people with DM and stroke, the long-term prognosis is worse, and there is a higher frequency of sequels, an increased risk of recurrence, and increased mortality within the first six months after the event.12
