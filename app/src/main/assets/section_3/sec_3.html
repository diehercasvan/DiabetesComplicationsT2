4. Risk factors for stroke

There are three types of risk factors for stroke:

  1. Non-modifiable13
  2. Modifiable13
  3. New risk factors13
  • Non-modifiable risk factors:
    • Age: Stroke is more common in patients over 60 years (risk increases with each decade of life and the presence of other risk factors).13
    Sex: Women have an estrogen-protective effect until menopause.13 In general terms, stroke is more common in men than in women.14 Mortality rates are higher among patients with history of low weight at birth.14
    Race: African Americans and Hispanic/Latino Americans have higher rates of stroke and mortality.14
    Genetic factors: Family history of stroke increases risk in about 30%.14 Children of stroke patients have a 1.5 higher risk of having stroke.13 The presence of the phosphodiesterase 4 (PDE4D) and arachidonate 5-lipoxygenaseactivating protein (ALOX5AP) genes increases the risk for stroke.13
  • Modifiable risk factors:
    • Hypertension: HTN is a risk factor for both cerebral infarction and intracerebral hemorrhage; the association between hypertension and stroke is strong, continuous, gradual, consistent, independent, predictive and etiologically significant.14 In the ICDANA study, a decrease of 6.5 mmHg in diastolic blood pressure was associated with a decrease between 30% and 40% for the risk of a first stroke.13 The use of certain angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors and angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARB) reduces the risk of stroke between 25% and 43%.13
    Smoking: Smoking is a strong risk factor for stroke.14 Smoking increases twice the risk for ischemic stroke.14
    Diabetes mellitus: the incidence of stroke in patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus (DM2) is 2 to 4 times higher than in the rest of the population.15 Stroke risk is greater in the group of 40-60 years, especially in women (RR 2.2 for women compared with 1.8 for men).15 In patients with diabetes, the most common types of stroke are atherothrombotic and lacunar.15 Several epidemiological studies have shown that the relationship between DM2 and hemorrhagic stroke is unclear.15
    The profiles of stroke among patients and the general population are different (Table 2).15 DM and hypertension are the most important factors associated with greater extension of subcortical stroke, with multiple lacunar infarcts being the most frequent radiological pattern.15
    Atrial fibrillation: AF is the most common supraventricular arrhythmia in clinical practice. AF patients have an incidence of ischemic stroke of 5% per year, which corresponds to an incidence between 2 and 7 times greater than in people without AF.16 Twenty percent of patients with AF also have DM.17
    Dyslipidemia: currently available information about relationship between stroke and dyslipidemia indicates that:
    • Some epidemiological studies have found a relationship between high cholesterol levels and the risk of ischemic stroke.14
    • Some studies have found an increased risk for hemorrhagic stroke with lower cholesterol levels.14
    • Some epidemiological studies have found an inverse relationship between HDL cholesterol levels and stroke.14
    • Studies to demonstrate the relationship between triglycerides and stroke are inconsistent.14
    Coronary heart disease: In patients with coronary heart disease, the risk of stroke is twice the risk of the general population.13
    Prior stroke: compared with patients with acute myocardial infarction, stroke patients are 3 to 4 times at higher risk of a second stroke within the first two years.13 Ten percent of patients with TIA will develop a stroke within the first three months; 50% within the first two days.13
    Other heart conditions: people with dilated cardiomyopathy, valvular heart disease (mitral valve prolapse, endocarditis and prosthetic valves), congenital heart defects (patent foramen ovale, atrial septal defects) have an increased risk of stroke.13 Twenty percent of all ischemic strokes are cardioembolic.13
    Atherosclerotic carotid disease: people with atherosclerotic stenotic carotid artery disease have an increased risk for stroke.14
    Dietary factors: are important as they are associated with higher prevalence of hypertension. They may include: salt intake, low potassium intake, overweight and obesity, and high consumption of alcohol.14
    Sedentary lifestyle: Physical inactivity is associated with an increased risk of total mortality, cardiovascular mortality, cardiovascular disease and stroke.14
    Obesity: In some studies, the total body fat was a strong predictor of stroke.14
    • Other less-documented risk factors for stroke include: migraine, metabolic syndrome, alcohol consumption, drug abuse, sleep breathing disorders (snoring, obstructive sleep apnea).14
    • New risk factors include: ehyperhomocysteinemia, increased lipoprotein(a), inflammation and infection (with elevated fibrinogen, serum amyloid, Lp-LPA 2 and interleukin-6), which have been associated with increased risk of stroke.14
