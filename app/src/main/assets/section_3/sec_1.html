2. Classification of stroke

Stroke is classified into: ischemic and hemorrhagic (Figure 1).1

  • Transient ischemic attack (TIA) is defined as symptoms of neurological deficit lasting less than 24 hours, without neuro-radiological evidence of acute brain injury.1,2,3
  • Cerebral infarction manifests as clinical, pathological and neuro-radiological signs with a duration exceeding 24 hours, secondary to impaired blood supply to brain tissue, which causes tissue necrosis.4
  • Intracerebral hemorrhage is the leakage of blood into the brain parenchyma due to spontaneous rupture of a blood vessel (not due to trauma) varying in size and location.5 Extravasated blood may accumulate into brain tissue (parenchymal) or ventricles (ventricular).5
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage is caused by extravasation of blood into the subarachnoid space usually due to rupture (non-traumatic) of a congenital aneurysm.6

Classification of stroke according to causative mechanism:

  • Thrombotic stroke: results from atherosclerosis-related stenosis of an intracranial or extracranial artery.6
  • Embolic stroke: occlusion of an artery caused by an embolus originated elsewhere into circulatory system: arteries, heart chambers or systemic circulation (if an atrial septum defect exists [paradoxical embolism]).6
  • Hemodynamic stroke: produced by low cardiac output, low blood pressure, or a phenomenon of “stolen blood flow” into arterial system.6
