6. Diagnosis of stroke

  • Initial approach
    • Stroke diagnosis is basically clinical18
    After clinical diagnosis confirmation, the time of onset of symptoms, personal history (risk factors), and drug treatment received should be established. General and neurological examination should also be performed.18 Clinical examination helps establish the affected vascular territory and the degree of neurological impairment (Neurological Rating Scales).18

  • Neuroimaging:
    • Computed Tomography (CT) Scan and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI). Neuroimaging studies, brain CT and MRI, help us to establish whether the stroke is ischemic or hemorrhagic (Table 4).18

    Other diagnostic techniques include: diffusion MRI (DWI), which has a specificity of 95% to 100% and a sensitivity of 88% to 100% for ischemic stroke; perfusion MRI (PWI) which detects a viable (or not) ischemic tissue (penumbra tissue) and potentially salvageable brain tissue; the magnetic resonance angiography, which has a sensitivity of 100% and a specificity of 95% for the diagnosis of intracranial arterial occlusion; and gradient-echo (T2) MRI, which increases the sensitivity for detection of intracerebral hemorrhagic stroke (acute or chronic).18
    Ultrasonographic studies: after diagnosing an ischemic stroke, the intracranial and extracranial circulation status must be assessed.18 For this purpose, supra-aortic trunks doppler and transcranial doppler (TCD) may be used.18
  • Laboratory tests
    • Blood tests: complete blood count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate, coagulation tests (partial thromboplastin time, prothrombin time and International Normalized Ratio [INR]).18 If thrombophilia is suspected, also include: antithrombin III, protein C, protein S (free and total), APC-r (Factor V Leiden), prothrombin gene mutation, anticardiolipin antibodies, lupus anticoagulant, B2 glycoprotein (B2 GP I), Heparin cofactor II, plasminogen, fibrinolytic system: tissue plasminogen activator (t-PA) and plasminogen activator inhibitor (PAI).18
    • Blood chemistry: liver and kidney function tests, blood glucose, glycated hemoglobin, lipid profile, uric acid, C reactive protein (CRP), differential proteins, calcium, phosphorus; In some patients, vitamin B12, folic acid and homocysteine levels.18
    • Hormonal tests: if endocrine pathology is suspected, evaluate thyroid hormones, catecholamines, basal lactic acid, etc.18
    • Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) study: if vasculitis, infection or subarachnoid hemorrhage is suspected.18
    • Chest x-ray: to evaluate the cardiac silhouette, lungs and aorta.18
    • ECG: perform a routine 13-lead ECG in all stroke patients.18
    • Other studies include: the 24-hour electrocardiographic monitoring (Holter), transthoracic echocardiography and, in some cases, transesophageal echocardiogram.18
